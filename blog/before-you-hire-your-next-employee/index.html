Before You Hire Your Next Employee, Do This First

By Dr. Connor Robertson

Photo by Scott Graham on Unsplash

I have watched a lot of business owners make the same expensive mistake. The business is growing, the owner is drowning, and the obvious answer feels like it is sitting right there: hire someone. So they post the job, run the interviews, bring someone on, and three months later they are more overwhelmed than they were before. Now they are managing a person who does not quite know what they are supposed to be doing, in addition to everything else they were already carrying.

The problem was never headcount. The problem was that the business did not have a system. And you cannot hire your way out of a systems problem.

This is one of the core ideas behind Built to Run, and it is the one that gets the most pushback when I first present it. Entrepreneurs are doers by nature. The idea of slowing down to document and systematize before adding capacity feels counterintuitive when everything is on fire. But the math is not complicated: a person dropped into an undocumented role becomes a dependency, not a solution. They become someone who can only do the job the way you showed them once, who checks with you before making any real decision, and who leaves you exposed the moment they quit.

The Bottleneck Is Almost Always the Owner

When I started looking honestly at the businesses I had built and acquired, I had to confront something uncomfortable. In most cases where growth had stalled or where the owner was burned out, the bottleneck was the owner. Not the market. Not the team. Not the economy. The owner had become the operating system of the business: every decision, every exception, every customer escalation, every vendor conversation flowed through them.

This is easy to fall into because it feels like control. But what it actually is is fragility. A business where the owner is the operating system cannot scale. It cannot be sold at a premium. It cannot survive the owner taking a two-week vacation, let alone a health event or a season of personal demands. The owner has not built a business. They have built a job that requires them to show up every day or everything stops.

Built to Run is the book I wrote to help business owners get out of that trap. And the first step out of it has nothing to do with hiring.

Documentation Is Not Bureaucracy

The word "documentation" makes people think of corporate policy manuals and three-ring binders nobody reads. That is not what I mean. What I mean is simple: every repeatable task in your business should have a written record of how it is done, why it is done that way, and what a good outcome looks like.

That is it. A one-page summary of how you onboard a new client. A checklist for closing out a project. A short video walking through how you handle a billing dispute. These do not need to be perfect, and they do not need to live in some elaborate software system. They just need to exist somewhere other than your head.

When you have that, something changes. You are no longer the only person who can do the work correctly. A new hire has something to reference. An existing team member can handle a situation in your absence without guessing. And when something goes wrong, you have a baseline to diagnose: was the process broken, or was the process not followed? Those are very different problems with very different solutions.

The Test I Give Every Business Owner

Here is a quick test I walk through in Built to Run. Think about the five or ten tasks you do most often in your business. Now ask yourself: if you had to be completely unreachable for two weeks starting tomorrow, which of those tasks would simply stop, get done wrong, or require someone to make a consequential guess?

The honest answer to that question is your documentation to-do list. Those are the systems that need to exist before you can meaningfully delegate. And here is what surprises most people when they actually do this exercise: the list is usually shorter than they expected. Most businesses have three to five core workflows that, if properly documented, would unlock the ability to delegate almost everything else.

The complexity business owners feel in their day-to-day is usually not complexity in the work itself. It is complexity created by the absence of systems, which means every situation feels like a unique problem that requires the owner's judgment, when really it is just a variation on something that has happened before and been handled informally dozens of times.

Delegation Requires a Standard

One of the things I see break down most often when owners try to delegate is the absence of a clear standard for what "done well" looks like. The owner hands off a task, the team member completes it, and the owner is frustrated because it is not what they envisioned. The team member is frustrated because they feel like they cannot win. And both of them blame the other person when the real problem is that the handoff never included a definition of success.

Effective delegation has three components: clarity on what needs to happen, clarity on why it matters, and clarity on what good output looks like. Without all three, you are not delegating. You are abdicating and hoping for the best. The difference matters enormously in the quality of work you get back and in whether your team members feel capable and trusted or confused and set up to fail.

In Built to Run, I walk through a delegation framework that I have used across multiple businesses. It is not complicated, but it requires intentionality, and the intentionality pays off in a team that can actually execute without you holding their hand through every step.

Then Hire

Once you have documented your core workflows and defined what good looks like, hiring becomes a completely different exercise. You are no longer hoping to find someone smart enough to figure out what the job is. You are looking for someone with the right disposition and skills to execute a role that already exists clearly. Onboarding becomes faster. Quality becomes more consistent. And when someone inevitably leaves, you have not lost the institutional knowledge of the role along with them.

The businesses that scale well are not the ones with the most talented people. They are the ones where ordinary people can do excellent work because the systems are clear. That is not an accident. It is a design decision that happens before the first job posting goes up.

If you are at the point in your business where you feel like you need help but are not sure what to hire for, or you have hired before and it has not gone the way you hoped, I would point you to Built to Run before you write another job description. The framework there will help you figure out exactly what needs to exist before you add a person to the equation.
